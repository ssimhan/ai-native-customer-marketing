# AI-Native Customer Marketing
## Core story

Most teams are using AI to move faster.

That is not the most interesting thing happening.

The more interesting shift is that AI lets you redesign the job itself.

For customer marketers, that means moving from:

- manual research to live intelligence
- scattered enablement to scalable education
- one-off work to reusable systems
- channel-by-channel comms to orchestrated growth loops

This is not “customer marketing, but 20% faster.”

This is customer marketing entirely rewritten around AI.

---
## Beat 1 — Cold open

If you work in marketing right now, you are probably using AI to go faster.

Faster writing. Faster summarizing. Faster content production. Faster first drafts. Faster follow-up.

And to be clear, that is useful.

I use AI for those things too.

But I also think that framing is a little misleading.

Because going faster at the wrong job is still the wrong job.

What has become much more interesting to me is not where AI saves me 20 minutes.

It is where AI changes what the job should even be in the first place.

And customer marketing is a particularly good place to see that happen.

Because customer marketing has always had this weird structural problem where an enormous amount of the work is real and important, but the operating model underneath it is often held together by manual stitching and one woman's caffeine addiction.

We stitch together customer context. We stitch together product signal. We stitch together educational content. We stitch together asks from sales, success, support, product, and execs. We stitch together stories that are spread across six systems and fourteen conversations. And we build this large quilt that covers the entire expansion motion at B2B companies.

So if you drop AI into that environment, yes, you can go a bit faster.

But the more meaningful thing is that you can start to ask a different question.

Not: how do I do the same work a little more efficiently?

But: if research, synthesis, drafting, and orchestration are suddenly much cheaper, what should this function actually become?

That is what I want to talk about.

---
## Beat 2 — Reframe

The old way to think about AI is pretty simple.

You take the existing workflow. You insert AI somewhere in the middle. You make it a little more efficient.

You write the email faster. You summarize the notes faster. You draft the deck faster. You analyze a spreadsheet faster.

That is AI as acceleration.

The AI-native way is different.

You start with the outcome you want.

Then you ask: if research is cheaper, if synthesis is cheaper, if finding patterns is cheaper, if turning chaos into structure is cheaper, if drafting and personalization are cheaper, what should the function itself look like now?

That is the lens I want to use for the rest of this talk.

Not “where did we save time?”

But “what work became newly possible?”

And more importantly for customer marketers: what work became newly scalable?

Because that is the piece I think matters.

A lot of customer marketing teams are not constrained by good ideas.

They are constrained by the fact that the function sits at the intersection of too many systems, too many stakeholders, too much fragmentation, and too little structure, and lets be real too little budget.

So once AI starts helping you collapse that fragmentation, you are not just speeding up tasks.

You are changing the design constraints of the function itself.

And the easiest way to follow the rest of this talk is with a very simple frame.

I think the AI-native customer marketing function has to get better at three things, in this order:

First, **detect** what is actually happening.

Second, **decide** what should happen next.

Third, **deliver** the right intervention at scale.

So the three examples I’m going to walk through are not random.

They ladder into each other.

Research is the detect layer. Operations is the decide layer. Education is the deliver layer.

That is the system redesign.

---
## Beat 3 — Why this matters to customer marketers

I lead customer marketing at Glean.

So my world spans the messy middle after the deal closes: activation, adoption, education, value realization, and expansion.

And I think customer marketing has a very specific problem.

We sit on top of a huge amount of signal, but very little of it arrives in a form we can use cleanly.

The data is fragmented. The content is fragmented. The customer context is fragmented. And the team ends up compensating with hustle.

We become the people who manually pull together the story. We're Tom Holland's spiderman keeping that ferry from tearing apart.

We manually figure out who needs what. We manually chase down examples. We manually convert raw release notes and JIRA tickets into customer-ready education. We manually decide which adoption signal matters and which one is noise. We manually translate scattered business asks into something we can actually prioritize.

That hustle often looks impressive from the outside.

A smart team can make a broken system look functional for a surprisingly long time.

But underneath it, the operating model is usually pretty fragile.

It depends on institutional memory. It depends on a few people who know where everything lives. It depends on someone remembering the right customer example. It depends on somebody interpreting three dashboards, two threads, a CSM comment, and a partridge in a pear tree and then turning that into an actual point of view.

What AI changed for us is not just speed.

It changed how much of that fragmentation we can collapse.

And once you collapse enough of it, the function starts to look different.

You stop being the team that mostly packages output.

You start becoming the team that interprets signal, builds systems, and shapes how the post-sales journey actually runs.

That is the shift.

And I think it is a particularly exciting shift for customer marketers, because our function has historically been treated as either:

- a nicer wrapper around enablement
- a lighter-weight version of product marketing
- a content arm for CS
- or the team that does “all the customer stuff that doesn’t have a home elsewhere”

I think AI gives us the opportunity to define the function more strategically than that.

## Beat 4 — Rewrite 1: research becomes live intelligence

The first rewrite is in research.

And the concrete outcome I am driving here is not “have AI make my dashboards.”

The outcome is: build a maturity model that can actually tell us who to educate, who to nudge, who to celebrate, and who is genuinely ready for expansion.

That is the real job.

This is the **detect** layer.

Before I can orchestrate anything, I need to know what is actually happening.

A lot of lifecycle marketing runs on vague definitions.

Power user. Healthy account. Expansion-ready. At-risk. Mature customer. Champion. Admin who needs support.

Those labels sound precise, but often they are inherited shortcuts.

At some point somebody needed a working definition, so one was created, and then it calcified.

One example for us was “power user.”

We had a rough definition.

The working definition was roughly a person who did 50 or more high-intent actions in a 28-day period.

Now, that sounds pretty good. It sounds specific. It sounds quantitative. It sounds like the kind of thing you can put in a dashboard and feel smart about.

But it still had a major weakness.

We did not actually know which behaviors predicted meaningful maturity.

And that matters, because if your definition is fuzzy, everything built on top of it is fuzzy too.

Your segmentation, messaging, education priorities are all weaker. Your maturity framework is weaker. Your expansion plays are based on proxies instead of signal. It's like having a leaky bucket and knowing its because your bucket is made of the wrong material.

So what changed?

Instead of treating this like a static marketing definition, we used Glean to help us work the problem more like a living intelligence problem.

We could surface prior analyses. We could pull up related thinking that already existed inside the company. We refined the question and accelerated the actual query work. And we kept interrogating the answer instead of settling for the first pass.

That is important, because in the old model, a question like this can take a long time to even get into motion.

First you have to remember whether anyone has studied it before. Then you ask around. Then you find a stale deck. Then you talk to data. Then you realize the original question was too broad. Then you wait because the data scientist is on vacation. Then maybe a few weeks later you get a large excel file of data.

What changed here was not just speed.

What changed was the shape of the interaction.

We were able to treat the question more dynamically.

Not “please answer this for me eventually.”

But “let’s work this problem until the concept itself gets smarter.”

And the output was not just a better report.

It actually changed the definition we use.

That was the important shift for me.

The value was not, “great, AI helped us analyze faster.”

The value was that a foundational concept inside our customer marketing system got smarter.

And once that definition improves, everything downstream improves too.

Now our segmentation is more meaningful. Now our education strategy can be tied to actual maturity steps. Now our “who should we nudge?” decisions become more grounded. Now our expansion thinking becomes less anecdotal. Now our maturity model is based on actual behavior rather than a convenient threshold.

So the outcome I am actually driving in this example is this:

I want a system that can identify where a customer or user is on the journey, what they need next, and whether they are moving toward deeper adoption and expansion.

That is a completely different level of utility than “we have a dashboard with a power user metric on it.”

This is where I think customer marketers should pay close attention.

Because historically, we often inherit definitions from sales, product, CS, or analytics.

And then we build programs on top of them.

But if AI makes it easier for us to test, challenge, and refine those definitions continuously, then research stops being a quarterly readout.

It becomes a live operating input.

Instead of saying, “Let’s revisit this framework in six months,” we can start building a function that learns as it runs.

And I think that is a profound change.

Because once research becomes live intelligence, customer marketing can stop operating mostly on lagging assumptions.

We can start operating on evolving understanding.

And once we can detect what is actually happening more accurately, the next question becomes obvious.

What should we do about it?

## Beat 5 — Rewrite 2: ops becomes structured decision support

The second rewrite is operations.

And the outcome I am driving here is this:

I want customer marketing to become a function that can take noisy signals from across the business and turn them into clear, prioritized actions that improve adoption, reduce drift, and support expansion.

That is the goal.

This is the **decide** layer.

Once I have better signal, I need a better decision layer sitting on top of it.

And this one is less glamorous, but I actually think it is one of the highest-leverage shifts.

A huge amount of customer marketing work used to show up as scattered asks.

A Slack message. An email thread. A comment in a doc. A half-formed request from sales or success. A discussion that starts as “quick question” and turns into a week-long side quest.

The work itself was not always hard.

The hard part was turning noise into something structured enough to act on.

And this is where I think a lot of customer marketers quietly lose leverage.

Not because they are doing low-value work.

But because they are spending too much of their time translating chaos.

Let me make that concrete.

You get looped into a thread because somebody wants help with an adoption issue.

What you actually receive is not a brief.

It is fragments.

A CS note. A couple of Slack messages. Maybe a product question. A vague sense that a certain cohort is not adopting. Someone wonders whether we should build content. Someone else says maybe this is actually onboarding. Another person mentions a support pattern. Then somebody asks for “a quick recommendation.”

At that point, the real work is not content.

The real work is sense-making.

What happened? Who is affected? Is this big or small? Is it a one-off or a pattern? Is this an education problem, a product problem, a messaging problem, or an instrumentation problem? What should happen next?

That interpretive work has historically been extremely manual.

And it often gets disguised as “collaboration.” But a lot of it is actually translation overhead.

AI helps here in a very specific way.

It can take messy, distributed context and turn it into a brief: what happened, why it matters, how big it is, what needs a response, which questions are still open, and who should do what next.

I used Glean in a few places where the underlying signal was noisy — long email threads, scattered Slack context, fragmented asks — and the unlock was not that I suddenly got a clever paragraph.

The unlock was that I got structure.

A summary of decisions. Owners. Open questions. Risks. Asks for leadership. Next steps.

That sounds operationally boring, but it is not boring when you live inside the function.

It is a leverage unlock.

Because once the work arrives in a structured form, you can prioritize it, route it, batch it, and learn from it.

You are no longer just responding to whoever pinged last.

You are building an operating layer.

And I think this matters because customer marketing often gets pulled into the business as a reactive service function.

A team that people throw things at. A team that is asked to “help with” whatever surfaced somewhere else. A team that absorbs ambiguity because no one else has the bandwidth to do the translation.

AI creates the opportunity to behave more like a strategic system.

Not just “we make things.”

But “we interpret signals, size the need, and help the organization act.”

I think that is a major difference.

Because when customer marketing becomes better at structured decision support, the function stops being downstream from everyone else’s confusion.

It starts becoming the team that can reduce confusion for the rest of the business.

And once you do that consistently, you gain a very different kind of credibility.

Not just creative or storytelling credibility, but operational credibility.

The kind where people start trusting your team not only to package information, but to make the work clearer.

And again, the end state I care about here is not “wow, we have better summaries.”

The end state is:

- faster triage
- clearer prioritization
- less reactive thrash
- better routing of the right intervention to the right problem
- and a customer marketing team that can actually influence outcomes instead of just absorb requests

One simple example of this mental shift is the difference between treating every incoming ask as a custom project versus treating each ask as a signal that should be normalized into a standard intake pattern.

If five different teams keep asking for the same kind of “customer story,” “adoption guidance,” “save this at-risk rollout,” or “help us explain this new behavior,” then the interesting question is not just how quickly can we answer.

The interesting question is: what system should exist so this stops arriving as chaos?

AI does not magically solve the org design problem.

But it gives you a much better way to convert scattered context into patterns you can actually operationalize.

So now we have a better detect layer, and a better decide layer.

The next question is: how do we actually deliver the right intervention at scale?

## Beat 6 — Rewrite 3: education becomes a system, not a pile

The third rewrite is education.

And the outcome I am driving here is very clear:

I want customers to get to value faster, with less dependence on one-to-one human intervention, and I want our educational system to keep up with the pace of the product.

That is the job.

This is the **deliver** layer.

Once I know what is happening, and I have more clarity on what should happen next, I need a scalable way to actually deliver the intervention.

And honestly, this one feels painfully familiar to almost every customer marketing team I know.

Like a lot of teams, we had accumulated a lot of helpful content in a very unhelpful format.

There were videos. There were docs. There were links in spreadsheets. There was tribal knowledge in the heads of our CSMs. There were internal recordings. There were ad hoc walkthroughs. There were Looms someone recorded six months ago that were still kind of useful but no one was sure if they were current.

In our case, we essentially had a Google Sheet full of around a hundred Loom links.

That is not an academy. That is not scaled education. That is an evidence locker where videos go to die.

Useful things existed. But they did not exist in a form that customers could actually learn from reliably.

So even when the raw material existed somewhere in Google Drive, it was hard to search, hard to trust, and hard to send to customers with confidence.

And that is a very normal customer marketing problem.

The team knows useful things. The company has useful raw material. But it does not exist in a form that scales.

You can sometimes get away with this when the company is smaller.

A strong CSM can say, “oh, I know the right video for this,” or “I remember someone did a walkthrough on that,” or “let me send you the one we used for that enterprise admin last quarter.”

Glean's context graph literally helps solve the enterprise search problem at scale.

But that model collapses as complexity grows.

Because what you actually need is not more content volume.

You need a system that turns scattered knowledge into structured learning.

This is where the AI-native shift became really interesting to me.

Content creation stops being the bottleneck.

The workflow can start to look more like this: record once, clean it up, re-voice it, brand it, structure it, make it searchable, add timestamps, feed it back into a knowledge base, and then make it easier to discover in the exact moment someone needs it.

That is a totally different model.

Instead of thinking, “how do we produce polished content from scratch every time?”

You start thinking, “how do we turn company learning into reusable learning objects?”

That changes the job.

Your CSMs and internal experts do not need to become polished content producers.

They need to become high-signal inputs and quality reviewers.

That is a radically more scalable model.

And it also changes the ambition of the function.

You stop asking: Can we produce enough content? Can we create enough videos? Can we keep up with releases? Can we manually package enough learning artifacts?

You start asking: Can we build an education system that gets better every time the company learns something?

That is a much bigger job. And a much more strategic one.

A concrete example of how I think about this:

Before, the flow looked like this:

Someone on the team knows something. They record a Loom. The Loom gets pasted into a sheet or a Slack thread. A few people know it exists. It may or may not get reused. A customer needs help later. Somebody hunts for it. There is uncertainty about whether it is current. The whole thing depends on memory and goodwill.

After, the flow starts looking more like this:

Someone records a useful explanation once. In our case we use an AI tool called Trupeer to help us do this. Their AI helps turn it into something cleaner and more structured video asset. That gets turned into a searchable asset with deeper metadata, timestamps, clearer framing, and reusable packaging. Then it becomes part of a system. Not a one-off artifact.

Once that happens, customer education starts to behave less like artisanal content production and more like infrastructure. [image here of artisanal cheese vs cheese block factory)

And that matters because customer marketing is increasingly being asked to support product adoption at a scale that human-led one-to-one education cannot absorb.

Especially in AI.

Because in AI products, customers are not just learning features. They are learning new patterns of work. They are learning new mental models. They are often learning what “good” even looks like. And then just for fun, that definition changes every month.

Which means the education burden is not small. And it is not static.

So the question for customer marketers becomes: can we build an education layer that is dynamic enough to keep up with the pace of the product and discoverable enough to actually change behavior?

That, to me, is the AI-native opportunity.

And the real outcome I want from that system is not “a nicer content library.”

It is:

- faster time-to-value
- less dependence on CSM heroics
- more trustworthy self-serve learning
- a cleaner bridge between what the company knows and what the customer can actually use

I also think this changes the identity of the team.

A lot of customer marketing and education work has historically been judged on output volume.

How many webinars did we run? How many docs did we publish? How many courses did we launch? How many videos did we create?

I increasingly think that is the wrong scoreboard.

A better scoreboard is: How searchable is our knowledge? How reusable is our learning? How quickly can we convert raw expertise into trustworthy education? How much less does this system depend on a particular person remembering where something lives?

That is a much more interesting measure of maturity.

## Beat 7 — The bigger pattern

If you step back, these examples all point to the same thing.

In each case, the workflow got better, yes.

But more importantly, the business outcome got clearer.

In research, I am trying to drive better maturity detection so we know who to educate, who to nudge, and who is truly ready for expansion.

In operations, I am trying to drive clearer action by turning fragmented asks into structured decisions and prioritized interventions.

In education, I am trying to drive faster time-to-value through a reusable, trustworthy learning system that does not depend on heroics.

That is why I keep saying this is not just about speed.

And that is also why I put these examples in this order.

First detect. Then decide. Then deliver.

Research becomes more live. Operations become more structured. Education becomes more reusable.

And customer marketing starts to look less like a content function and more like an orchestration layer.

That is the phrase I keep coming back to.

An orchestration layer.

Because the question is no longer, “Can we make this asset?”

The question becomes, “What should happen because this signal just changed?”

A customer starts adopting a feature. An admin stalls. A champion shows unusual engagement. A region is lagging. A use case is taking off. A new feature lands but the intended audience is not moving.

Those are not just analytics observations.

They are triggers.

Triggers for different kinds of education, outreach, proof collection, executive communication, in-product nudges, community moments, or expansion motion.

That is where customer marketing gets more strategic.

We stop acting like a downstream service desk for content and support.

We start acting like the team that connects signal to action.

And I think that is a much more interesting future for the function.

Because it means the function is no longer defined mainly by the assets it ships.

It is defined by how well it helps the company learn and act across the post-sales lifecycle.

## Beat 8 — The function becomes compounding

The next shift is that AI-native customer marketing compounds.

In the old model, the team gets credit for heroics.

A great webinar. A great deck. A last-minute customer save. A really smart person who somehow stitched together a great story from a bunch of broken inputs.

And those things matter, but they do not necessarily compound.

In the new model, the bigger win is building systems that get smarter and more reusable over time.

A better definition of maturity, a better content pipeline, a better map from product signal to lifecycle action. A better way to identify which educational interventions actually work. A better feedback loop between product behavior and customer communication.

Each one makes the next one easier.

So the output is not just more assets.

It is more leverage.

And I think that is a useful way for customer marketers to evaluate their own work.

Not just, “did we ship something good?”

But, “did we build a system that makes the next thing easier, smarter, or more targeted?”

Did we reduce dependency on tribal knowledge? Did we make a core definition smarter? Did we make a learning asset more reusable? Did we convert recurring chaos into a standard operating pattern? Did we make it easier for the next person to do good work?

That is compounding.

And once you start viewing the function that way, the roadmap starts to change.

You invest differently. You instrument differently. You prioritize differently. You hire differently.

Because you are no longer just trying to do more work.

You are trying to redesign how the work scales.

## Beat 9 — What it actually takes

Now, none of this happens just because you bought an AI tool. Sadly not even Glean. 

To do this well, you need three things.

First, instrumentation.

If your data and signals are a mess, AI will help you produce polished confusion faster.

I think this is one of the easiest traps to fall into.

The output can look sophisticated. The writing can look polished. The summary can look smart. The segmentation can look precise.

But if the underlying inputs are weak, the result is just more articulate confusion.

So instrumentation matters.

Second, judgment.

You still need humans who know what good is, what a useful brief is, what crisp education looks like, and what should never be automated.

AI can help you generate options. It can help you structure work. It can help you find patterns. But it cannot own the standard of quality for the function.

That is still our job.

Third, systems thinking.

If you only use AI as a one-off assistant, you will get one-off gains.

If you use it to redesign the flow of work, you get a function that scales exponentially.

That is the real shift.

And I think this is especially important for customer marketers.

Because our function can easily get trapped in the language of outputs: campaigns, webinars, decks, nurtures, launches, assets, enablement pieces.

But the more AI-native version of the function is really about systems:

systems for learning, systems for education, systems for prioritization, systems for signal interpretation, systems for turning customer behavior into action.

That does not make the craft less important.

If anything, it makes the craft more important.

Because once AI lowers the cost of producing mediocre work, your actual advantage becomes better judgment about what good work should be and better systems for making that good work repeatable.

That is where I think the role gets more interesting, not less.

Not faster production for its own sake. Not more content for its own sake. Not more automation for its own sake.

A better shape of work.

A function that can sense more, learn faster, personalize more intelligently, and scale without collapsing under manual overhead.

That is what I mean by AI-native customer marketing.

And I think the opportunity in front of customer marketers is not just to adopt AI.

It is to redesign the operating model of the function itself.

To move from hustle to systems. From reactive service work to signal-driven orchestration. From isolated assets to compounding leverage.

To become the team that helps the company learn faster after the deal closes.

To become the team that turns fragmented signal into coordinated action.

To become the team that does not just ship content, but shapes how adoption and expansion actually happen.

That is the shift.
